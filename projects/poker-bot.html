What It Does

Four CFR-family solvers compute Nash-approximate strategies from Kuhn Poker to Leduc Poker, a 24x larger game tree. DCFR drives exploitability to 0.0008 chips per game, 33% below vanilla CFR at the same 10,000 iterations. A Bayesian opponent model with sequential leak detection switches from defensive to exploitative play in real time. A Glosten-Milgrom module ties bluff frequency to how bid-ask spreads form in markets.

It spans a 24x range of game tree size, from Kuhn Poker with 12 information sets to Leduc Poker with 288. A playable command line bot runs the solvers, opponent model, and trading bridge together and adapts as it plays.

How It Works

Four CFR Solvers

Vanilla CFR, CFR+, DCFR, and external sampling MCCFR each minimize counterfactual regret toward a Nash equilibrium. At 10,000 iterations on Kuhn, DCFR reaches 0.0008 exploitability, against 0.001 for CFR+ and 0.0012 for Vanilla, and all four recover the analytical Kuhn equilibrium to within tolerance.

The Bayesian Opponent Model

A per information set Dirichlet posterior tracks opponent action frequencies and updates by conjugate Bayesian inference. A sequential probability ratio test decides whether the opponent deviates from GTO, and only once the evidence crosses the threshold does a confidence gated mixer blend GTO and exploitative play.

The Glosten-Milgrom Bridge

The module maps a strong hand to an informed trader and a bluff to a noise trader, tying the Nash bluff frequency to the adverse selection component of the bid-ask spread. It outputs fair spreads derived from the CFR equilibrium and sizes positions with the half Kelly criterion.

Results

DCFR drives exploitability to 0.0008 chips per game, 33% below vanilla CFR at the same 10,000 iterations. The tree traversal best response is cross validated against brute force on Kuhn to within 0.002, and CFR+ converges on the 24x larger Leduc tree with monotonically decreasing exploitability.
