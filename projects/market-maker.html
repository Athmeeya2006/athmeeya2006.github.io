What It Does

A C++ limit order book exposed to Python through pybind11. Benchmarks six market-making algorithms across 10,000 round simulations under four market regimes. After a sudden regime shift, the sliding-window Exp3 re-finds the best quote in 1 round versus 161 for a static learner, a 161x faster recovery. Cumulative regret holds at 63% of the O(sqrt(T K ln K)) worst-case bound, so it provably never trails the best fixed quote by much.

The goal is bounded regret under parameter drift and adversarial order flow without modeling the environment. The Exp3 family makes no assumptions about the reward process and still guarantees sublinear regret, and the benchmark measures whether that guarantee holds across the four regimes.

How It Works

The C++ Order Book

The matching engine is roughly 760 lines of C++ with price time priority and FIFO queues at each level. A pybind11 layer exposes it to about 3,800 lines of Python. The engine derives order flow imbalance, rolling realized volatility, volume weighted average price, and depth, which feed directly into the learning algorithms.

Six Algorithms, Four Regimes

The benchmark runs Exp3, Exp3 with the doubling trick, sliding-window Exp3, contextual EXP4, the Avellaneda-Stoikov model baseline, and a fixed-spread baseline across 10,000 round simulations. Four regimes, calm, volatile, illiquid, and informed, transition abruptly or gradually to test how each algorithm adapts.

Measuring Regret

RegretTracker computes external regret versus the best fixed spread in hindsight using per round counterfactuals, with rolling windows and bootstrap confidence intervals that make the convergence curves statistically interpretable.

Results

After a sudden regime shift, sliding-window Exp3 re-finds the best quote in 1 round versus 161 for a static learner, a 161x faster recovery.

Cumulative regret reaches 198.58 against an O(sqrt(T K ln K)) worst-case bound of 315.93, that is 63% of the bound, so the strategy provably never trails the best fixed quote by much.
