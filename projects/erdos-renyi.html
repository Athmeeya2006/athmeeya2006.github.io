Erdos and Renyi Random Graph and Financial Contagion

CCNSB Lab, IIIT Hyderabad
VIEW CODE →

What It Does

A Monte Carlo study validates three classical phase transition scaling laws. Runs over 10,980 simulations. Implements a graph generator 1,000x faster than the naive approach. Models financial contagion on interbank networks. Shows S&P 500 correlation structure deviates from a random graph null by 30 standard deviations.

It uses the Erdos and Renyi random graph as a null model, confirms the scaling laws by simulation, then extends the framework to failure cascades across interbank exposures.

How It Works

Fast Graph Generation

The naive generator flips a Bernoulli coin for every pair of nodes, which is O(n squared) and infeasible at scale. The Batagelj Brandes algorithm samples the skip distance to the next realized edge from a geometric distribution and jumps straight to it. The empirical speedup is roughly 1,000x at 10,000 nodes, and a 500,000 node graph completes in under a second.

Three Scaling Laws

Giant component emergence, the cluster size power law, and finite size scaling are each validated quantitatively. Finite size scaling runs across 10,980 simulations over three system sizes, and the fitted logarithmic slopes match the theoretical predictions.

Financial Contagion

Three contagion models run on interbank networks: a bond percolation cascade equivalent to the SIR epidemic model, the Watts threshold cascade, and DebtRank. A phase diagram maps cascade size across 100 combinations of transmissibility and equity buffer, separating the safe region from the systemic crisis region.

Results

Across 10,980 simulations the empirical scaling exponents match theory. A Pearson correlation network over 100 S&P 500 stocks deviates from the Erdos and Renyi null by a Z score exceeding 30 standard deviations, rejecting the hypothesis that the correlation structure is random.
